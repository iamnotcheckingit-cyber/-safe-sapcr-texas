Missed Custody Court Date in Texas

What to do immediately if you missed a SAPCR hearing

Missing a custody court date in Texas can lead to a default judgment, which may significantly affect your parental rights. If this just happened, time is critical.

What Happens If You Miss a Custody Hearing in Texas?

If you miss a custody hearing in Texas, the judge may proceed without you and enter a default judgment. The other parent can present their case unopposed, and the court may award custody, set visitation, and order child support — all without your input. The judgment is legally binding.

If you fail to appear at a scheduled SAPCR hearing:

  • The judge may proceed without you
  • The other party can present their case unopposed
  • A default judgment may be entered granting the other party everything they requested
  • Custody, visitation, and child support can all be decided in your absence

These decisions are legally binding even though you were not present.

First Step: Check If a Default Judgment Was Entered in Texas

Contact the court clerk immediately to check whether a final order was signed. In Harris County, you can check case status online at hcdistrictclerk.com. Note the exact date the order was signed — this starts your deadlines for challenging it.

Immediately:

  1. Contact the court clerk for the court where your case is pending
  2. Request the case status — ask specifically if a final order has been signed
  3. Get a copy of any orders entered — you need to know exactly what was decided
  4. Note the date the order was signed — this starts your deadlines running

If the Judgment Was Just Entered (Within 30 Days)

You may still have time to file a Motion for New Trial under TRCP Rule 329b. This is your best and fastest option. You must show:

  • You didn't intentionally miss court
  • You have a valid defense to the custody claims
  • Granting a new trial won't unfairly prejudice the other party

Deadline: The motion must be filed within 30 days of the judgment being signed. If the court doesn't rule within 75 days, the motion is denied by operation of law.

If More Than 30 Days Have Passed

Your options become more limited but are not gone:

  • Restricted Appeal (within 6 months): Available if you did not participate in the hearing and there is error apparent on the face of the record.
  • Bill of Review (within 4 years): A separate legal action to set aside the judgment. You must prove a meritorious defense, that you were prevented from presenting it, and that it wasn't your fault.
  • Modification (Chapter 156): If you can't set aside the judgment, you may be able to modify custody based on changed circumstances — but this accepts the original order as valid.

Valid Reasons for Missing a Custody Court Date in Texas

Courts may consider:

  • Medical emergencies (documented)
  • Lack of proper notice of the hearing
  • Incorrect scheduling information from the court
  • Being incarcerated and unable to appear
  • Natural disaster or other circumstances beyond your control

Simply forgetting or choosing not to attend is much harder to overcome. Courts expect parties to treat custody hearings seriously.

What NOT to Do

  • Do not wait. Every day that passes reduces your options.
  • Do not assume it will fix itself. Default judgments are legally binding.
  • Do not ignore court orders already entered. Violating a custody order can result in contempt charges.
  • Do not stop paying child support if it was ordered — even if you plan to challenge the judgment.

How to Prevent Missing a Custody Hearing in Texas

  • Always file an answer when served with a SAPCR petition
  • Track all court dates on a calendar with reminders
  • Keep your contact information updated with the court clerk
  • If you cannot attend, contact the court in advance to request a continuance
  • File electronically for free through eFileTexas.gov
