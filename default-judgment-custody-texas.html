Default Judgment in Texas Custody Cases

What it is, how it happens, and how to fight back

What Is a Default Judgment in a SAPCR?

A default judgment occurs when one party in a lawsuit fails to respond or appear, and the court enters judgment in favor of the other party. In a Texas Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship (SAPCR), a default judgment can award sole managing conservatorship, restrict visitation to supervised-only, establish child support obligations, and terminate parental rights — all without the respondent parent ever being heard.

Under Texas law, a respondent who is properly served with a SAPCR generally must file an Answer by 10:00 AM on the first Monday after 20 days from service. If no answer is filed, the petitioner may request a default judgment.

What Happens If You Don't Respond to a SAPCR

If you fail to file an answer or appear, the court may enter a default judgment granting the petitioner everything requested — sole managing conservatorship, restricted visitation, child support, and more. The judgment is legally binding even though you were not present.

Why Default Judgments Happen: Common Causes

Default judgments can result from a respondent's own inaction, but they can also result from problems with service of process. Common scenarios include:

  • Service at a wrong address: The petition is served at an address where the respondent does not live.
  • Service by publication without due diligence: The petitioner claims the respondent cannot be located, but made no genuine effort to find them.
  • False Certificate of Last Known Address: Under Texas Rule 239a, the attorney certifies a stale or false address, enabling service by posting to a location the respondent will never see.
  • Failure to file return of service: Personal service is completed but the return is never filed, allowing the attorney to later claim the respondent was never found.

Your Constitutional Rights

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution guarantees due process of law, which includes the right to notice and an opportunity to be heard before a court deprives you of a protected interest. Parental rights are a fundamental constitutional interest. A default judgment entered without proper notice may be void or voidable as a violation of due process.

How to Set Aside a Default Judgment: Bill of Review

In Texas, the primary remedy for setting aside a default judgment obtained through fraud is a Bill of Review. This is an equitable proceeding filed in the same court that entered the original judgment. To succeed, you must prove three elements:

  1. Meritorious defense: You must show that you had a valid defense to the underlying SAPCR that, if presented, would have produced a different result.
  2. Prevented from presenting your defense: You must prove that fraud, accident, or wrongful act of the opposing party prevented you from presenting your defense. This is where extrinsic fraud — such as a false Certificate of Last Known Address — is alleged.
  3. No negligence on your part: You must show that your failure to appear was not due to your own negligence.

Timeline and Deadlines

  • Four-year statute of limitations: A Bill of Review generally must be filed within four years of the judgment date.
  • Motion for New Trial: If you learn of the judgment within 30 days, you may file a Motion for New Trial instead.
  • Restricted Appeal: If the judgment was entered within the last six months and you did not participate in the case, a restricted appeal may be available.

Act immediately upon learning of a default judgment. Courts consider delay in filing as potential evidence of negligence.

Steps to Take If You Have a Default Judgment Against You

  1. Obtain a copy of the default judgment and all related filings from the district clerk.
  2. Request the Certificate of Last Known Address filed under Rule 239a.
  3. Check whether a return of service was filed and what address was used.
  4. Document your actual address at the time of the judgment.
  5. Consult a family law attorney experienced in Bills of Review.
  6. Consider filing a complaint with the State Bar of Texas if the attorney knowingly filed false documents.
  7. If applicable, report the matter to law enforcement for potential criminal charges.

Common Reasons Default Judgments Happen in SAPCR Cases

  • Not properly served: Papers were sent to a wrong or outdated address, and you never received them.
  • Served but didn't understand the paperwork: The citation and petition can be confusing, and some respondents don't realize they must file a written answer with the court by a specific deadline.
  • Assumed the case would not proceed: Some respondents believe the petitioner will drop the case, or that an informal agreement makes the lawsuit unnecessary.
  • Missed the answer deadline: The 20-day window (TRCP Rule 99) passes quickly, especially if you are served while traveling or dealing with other life circumstances.
  • Missed a court date: Even if you filed an answer, failing to appear at a hearing can result in a post-answer default.
  • Service documents were withheld: In some cases, the petitioner or their agents may deliberately prevent the respondent from retaining the service documents.

Can You Reopen a SAPCR Case After Default?

Yes — but it becomes more difficult over time. Courts prefer finality, especially in cases involving children who have adjusted to a custody arrangement. To succeed, you must act quickly and show a legitimate reason for your absence or failure to respond.

The strongest argument for setting aside a default judgment is that you were never properly served. Improper service means the court may not have had personal jurisdiction over you, and a judgment entered without jurisdiction is void. A void judgment can be challenged at any time — there is no deadline.

If service was technically proper but you missed the deadline for other reasons, your options narrow as time passes. A Motion for New Trial (30 days), Restricted Appeal (6 months), and Bill of Review (4 years) each have progressively higher burdens of proof.

A modification under Chapter 156 is not the right remedy for a judgment obtained through fraud — it accepts the judgment as valid. A Bill of Review is the correct remedy because it attacks the judgment itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I lose custody by default in Texas?

Yes. If you do not file an answer or appear at a hearing, the court can grant sole managing conservatorship to the other party, restrict your visitation to supervised-only, and set child support — all without your input.

What if I didn't know about the court date?

You may still have options, especially if you were not properly served. Obtain the return of service and Certificate of Last Known Address from the district clerk to determine if service was defective. If the address certified was wrong, the judgment may be void.

Can a default judgment be reversed?

Yes, but only if you act within the applicable deadlines and meet the legal requirements. The strongest cases involve defective or fraudulent service of process. Consult a family law attorney immediately.

What is the difference between modifying and setting aside a default judgment?

A modification (Chapter 156) accepts the judgment as valid and changes it based on changed circumstances. A Bill of Review attacks the judgment as fraudulently obtained and seeks to void it entirely. For fraud cases, the Bill of Review is the correct remedy.
