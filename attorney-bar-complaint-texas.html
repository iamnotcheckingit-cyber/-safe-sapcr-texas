How to File a Bar Complaint Against a Texas Attorney

State Bar Grievance Guide

When to File a Grievance

The State Bar of Texas accepts grievances against licensed attorneys who violate the Texas Disciplinary Rules of Professional Conduct. Conduct that may warrant a grievance includes:

  • Filing false or misleading documents with the court
  • Making false statements of material fact to a tribunal (Rule 3.03(a)(1))
  • Failing to communicate with clients or opposing parties as required
  • Mishandling client funds
  • Conflicts of interest
  • Neglect or abandonment of a case
  • Conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice

Step 1: Gather Documentation

Before filing, collect all relevant documents:

  • Court filings and docket entries
  • Correspondence (emails, letters) with the attorney
  • Contracts or fee agreements
  • Evidence of the misconduct (false certificates, missing returns of service, etc.)
  • A timeline of events
  • Names and contact information of witnesses

Step 2: Identify the Attorney

You will need the attorney's full legal name and State Bar of Texas identification number. You can look up any Texas attorney at the State Bar's website. For example, if filing against an attorney involved in a SAPCR case, the bar number is typically listed on all court filings.

Step 3: File the Grievance

There are several ways to file:

  • Online: Through the State Bar of Texas website at the Office of Chief Disciplinary Counsel portal
  • By mail: Office of Chief Disciplinary Counsel, State Bar of Texas, P.O. Box 12487, Austin, TX 78711-2487
  • By fax: (512) 427-4169
  • By phone: (800) 932-1900 (toll-free in Texas)

The grievance should include a detailed, factual description of the attorney's conduct, organized chronologically. Attach copies (not originals) of all supporting documents.

Step 4: The Classification Process

After you file, the grievance goes through a classification process:

  1. Initial review: The Office of Chief Disciplinary Counsel reviews your grievance to determine if it alleges conduct that, if true, would violate the Texas Disciplinary Rules of Professional Conduct.
  2. Classification: The grievance is either classified as a "complaint" (moving forward for investigation) or as an "inquiry" (not alleging a rule violation). You will receive written notice of the classification.
  3. Appeal: If your grievance is classified as an inquiry and you disagree, you may appeal the classification.

Step 5: Investigation and Resolution

If classified as a complaint:

  1. The attorney is notified and given an opportunity to respond.
  2. The Office of Chief Disciplinary Counsel investigates the matter.
  3. The matter may be resolved through:
    • Dismissal (insufficient evidence)
    • Private reprimand
    • Public reprimand
    • Suspension of the attorney's license
    • Disbarment

The timeline for resolution varies. Simple matters may be resolved in a few months; complex cases may take a year or more.

Other Regulatory Complaints

Depending on the attorney's conduct, you may also file complaints with:

  • Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC): If the attorney is also a licensed real estate agent or if the misconduct involves real estate transactions.
  • Judicial Branch Certification Commission (JBCC): If the misconduct involves a process server, court reporter, or other court-certified professional.
  • Local law enforcement: If the conduct constitutes a criminal offense, such as filing a false document with the court.
  • Harris County District Attorney: For potential perjury or fraud charges.

Tips for Filing an Effective Grievance

  • Be factual and specific. Avoid emotional language.
  • Include dates, case numbers, and document references.
  • Organize your complaint chronologically.
  • Reference specific Disciplinary Rules you believe were violated.
  • Include copies of all supporting documents.
  • Keep copies of everything you submit.
  • Follow up if you do not receive a response within 60 days.
