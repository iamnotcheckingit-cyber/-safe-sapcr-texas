Understanding SAPCR Cases

What is a SAPCR?

SAPCR stands for Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship. It is the legal proceeding in Texas used to:

  • Establish paternity
  • Determine custody (conservatorship)
  • Set visitation schedules (possession and access)
  • Order child support
  • Modify existing custody orders

SAPCR cases are governed by the Texas Family Code, Title 5.

What is the difference between custody and conservatorship?

In Texas, the term "custody" is not used legally. Instead, Texas uses conservatorship:

  • Managing Conservator: Has the right to make major decisions about the child (education, medical, etc.)
  • Possessory Conservator: Has visitation rights but limited decision-making authority
  • Joint Managing Conservators: Both parents share decision-making responsibilities
  • Sole Managing Conservator: One parent has exclusive decision-making authority
What is a default judgment in a custody case?

A default judgment is a court ruling made when one party fails to respond to legal proceedings. In custody cases, this can result in:

  • Full custody awarded to the petitioner
  • Termination of parental rights
  • Child support obligations
  • Loss of visitation rights

Default judgments in SAPCR cases are particularly devastating because they affect children and can be difficult to overturn.

Service of Process

What is service of process?

Service of process is the legal procedure of delivering court documents to a party, notifying them of legal action. In Texas, valid methods include:

  • Personal service: Hand-delivery by a constable or process server
  • Substituted service: Leaving documents with someone at the residence
  • Service by publication: Publishing notice in a newspaper (used when person cannot be located)
  • Service by posting: Posting at the courthouse

Proper service is a constitutional due process requirement. Without it, a judgment may be void.

What is a Certificate of Last Known Address?

Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 239a, before a court can authorize service by publication or posting, the plaintiff's attorney must file a sworn certificate stating the defendant's last known address.

The problem: Currently, there is no requirement to verify this address is accurate. Attorneys can certify outdated or incorrect addresses without consequence, enabling fraudulent service.

The SAFE SAPCR Act would require due diligence verification before filing this certificate.

What is extrinsic fraud and abuse of process?

Extrinsic fraud and abuse of process occurs when:

  • A party is prevented from presenting their case through deception
  • False information is provided about a party's location
  • Legal procedures are misused to achieve an improper purpose
  • An attorney files a false Certificate of Last Known Address
  • Proper notice is deliberately withheld to obtain a default judgment

Extrinsic fraud violates constitutional due process rights and may constitute fraud upon the court.

Fighting Back: Bill of Review

What is a Bill of Review?

A Bill of Review is an equitable proceeding to set aside a final judgment. It is filed in the same court that issued the original judgment.

In Texas, you must prove three elements:

  1. Meritorious defense: You have a valid defense to the original claims
  2. Prevented from presenting: Fraud, accident, or wrongful act of the opposing party prevented you from presenting your defense
  3. No negligence: You were not negligent in failing to respond to the lawsuit

For cases involving fraudulent service, the fraud element is typically satisfied if you can prove you were not properly notified.

How long do I have to file a Bill of Review?

In Texas, a Bill of Review generally must be filed within four years of the date the judgment was signed.

However, courts may consider equitable factors in cases involving:

  • Extrinsic fraud (fraud that prevented you from participating)
  • Discovery of fraud after the limitation period
  • Ongoing concealment of the judgment

Important: Consult an attorney immediately if you believe you're a victim of fraudulent service. Time limits are strictly enforced.

Can I file a Bill of Review myself (pro se)?

Yes, you can file a Bill of Review pro se (without an attorney). However, be aware that:

  • Courts hold pro se litigants to the same standards as attorneys
  • Procedural errors can result in dismissal
  • Evidence requirements are strict
  • The opposing party will likely have an attorney

What evidence do I need to prove fraudulent service?

Gather as much evidence as possible, including:

  • Proof of your actual address: Utility bills, lease agreements, bank statements, driver's license
  • Evidence opposing counsel knew your address: Emails, text messages, prior court filings, prior service at correct address
  • Proof you weren't at the served address: Work records, travel records, GPS data, witness statements
  • Evidence of the false certificate: Copy of the Certificate of Last Known Address with incorrect information
  • Prior proceedings: Records showing you appeared when properly served

Document everything with dates. Affidavits from witnesses can be powerful evidence.

The SAFE SAPCR Act

What is the SAFE SAPCR Act?

The SAFE SAPCR Act (Service Accountability and Fraud Elimination) is proposed legislation to reform service of process requirements in Texas family court cases. Key provisions include:

  • Mandatory constable service for initial SAPCR citations
  • Due diligence certification before alternative service
  • Automated case registry to notify parties of filings
  • Expedited Bill of Review hearings for fraudulent service claims
  • Criminal penalties for knowingly filing false address certificates

I Never Received Custody Papers

I never received custody papers in Texas. Can I still fight the judgment?

Yes, you may be able to fight the judgment if you were never properly served. Under Texas law, you have a constitutional right to notice of legal proceedings against you. If you never received the custody papers (citation), the judgment may be:

  • Void - If service was completely defective (never attempted or served to wrong person)
  • Voidable - If service had technical defects that can be challenged

Your primary remedy is a Bill of Review. You must prove you weren't negligent and that fraud, accident, or wrongful act prevented proper service. Gather evidence of your actual address at the time - utility bills, lease agreements, bank statements.

Act quickly: Time limits apply. Consult an attorney immediately.

I was served at the wrong address in Texas. What can I do?

If you were served at an address where you didn't live, this may constitute extrinsic fraud - especially if the other party knew your correct address. Here's what you can do:

  1. Get the Return of Service - Request this document from the court clerk showing where service was allegedly made
  2. Get the Certificate of Last Known Address - Under Texas Rule 239a, attorneys must certify your last known address before alternative service
  3. Gather address proof - Collect documents proving where you actually lived (lease, utilities, license, mail)
  4. Document opposing party's knowledge - Prior court filings, emails, texts showing they knew your real address
  5. File a Bill of Review - This is how you challenge a judgment obtained through improper service

I didn't know about my custody case until after the judgment. Is this legal?

No, it violates your constitutional due process rights. The 14th Amendment guarantees you notice and an opportunity to be heard before the government can deprive you of a protected interest - including your parental rights.

However, courts assume service was proper unless you prove otherwise. If you didn't know about the case, it's likely because:

  • You were served by publication (newspaper) while the other party knew your address
  • A false Certificate of Last Known Address was filed
  • Service was made to an old address despite the petitioner knowing your current one
  • The process server made a false return claiming personal service

This is called a collateral attack on the judgment. You'll need to file a Bill of Review and prove the service was fraudulent or defective. See our resources for step-by-step guidance.

How do I respond to a SAPCR in Texas?

If you've been properly served with a SAPCR (Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship), here's how to respond:

  1. Note your deadline - You typically have until 10:00 AM on the first Monday after 20 days from service to file an Answer
  2. File an Answer - This is your formal written response denying or admitting the claims. Use the court's forms or hire an attorney
  3. File a Counter-Petition - If you want to request custody, visitation, or support, file your own claims
  4. Request disclosures - Ask for the other party's evidence and information
  5. Attend all hearings - Missing hearings can result in default judgment against you

Getting Help

I just found out about a default judgment against me. What do I do?

Act immediately. Time limits are strict. Here's what to do:

  1. Get a copy of the judgment from the court clerk
  2. Get the service documents - the citation and return of service
  3. Document your actual address at the time of alleged service
  4. Consult with an attorney - many offer free consultations
  5. Consider filing a Bill of Review if you were not properly served
  6. Check for pending appeals deadlines
