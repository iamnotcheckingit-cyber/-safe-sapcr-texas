What Happens If You Don't Respond to a SAPCR in Texas?

Your deadline, the risks, and what you can still do

If you've been served with a SAPCR (Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship) in Texas and do nothing, the case will move forward without you. This can result in a default judgment that affects custody, visitation, and parental rights.

What Is a SAPCR?

A SAPCR is the legal proceeding in Texas that determines custody (conservatorship), visitation (possession and access), and child support. It is governed by the Texas Family Code and filed in district court.

Your Deadline to Respond to a SAPCR in Texas

Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 99, you must file a written answer by 10:00 AM on the first Monday after 20 days from the date you were served. Missing this deadline puts you at risk of a default judgment that can affect your custody and parental rights.

What Happens If You Don't Respond to a SAPCR in Texas?

If you don't respond to a SAPCR in Texas, the court may enter a default judgment awarding the other parent primary custody, restricting your visitation, and setting child support obligations — all without hearing your side. Can You Fix a Missed SAPCR Deadline in Texas?

Yes, but your options depend on timing. Within 30 days, file a Motion for New Trial. Within 6 months, a Restricted Appeal may be available. Within 4 years, file a Bill of Review. If the judgment is void due to defective service, there is no time limit to challenge it.

Common Reasons People Don't Respond to a SAPCR in Texas

  • Didn't understand the paperwork or realize a deadline applied
  • Thought the case wasn't serious or would go away
  • Never actually received the papers (improper service)
  • Were confused about which court or what to file
  • Couldn't afford an attorney and didn't know about pro se options

Why Responding Matters — Even a Simple Answer

Filing even a basic general denial answer:

  • Prevents default — the court cannot enter judgment without a hearing
  • Preserves your rights — you get to present your side
  • Forces the case to be decided on evidence, not assumptions

You can represent yourself pro se under TRCP Rule 7. Free forms are available at TexasLawHelp.org and you can file electronically for free through eFileTexas.gov.
