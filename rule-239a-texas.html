Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 239a

Certificate of Last Known Address in Default Judgments

What Rule 239a Requires

Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 239a requires the party taking a default judgment to certify the defendant's last known mailing address to the clerk in writing at or immediately before the judgment is signed. The clerk must then mail written notice of the signed judgment to the defendant at the certified address.

The notice includes the case number, case style, court name, party names, and the date the judgment was signed.

Key Aspects of TRCP 239a

  • Certification: The party requesting the default judgment (or their attorney) must file a certificate listing the defendant's last known mailing address.
  • Notice Delivery: The court clerk must immediately mail notice of the signed judgment to the defendant at the certified address.
  • Notice Contents: The notice includes the case number, style, court, party names, and the date the judgment was signed.
  • Non-Compliance: Failure to comply with this rule does not affect the finality of the default judgment itself, though it may be relevant in efforts to set aside the judgment.
  • Purpose: The rule is intended to ensure defendants are aware of a judgment against them, even if they did not answer the lawsuit.

The Verification Gap

Rule 239a contains no requirement that the attorney verify the address is accurate or current. The attorney can file a certification with any address, including one that is years or decades old, with no obligation to confirm the respondent still lives there. The court accepts the certification at face value.

This means an attorney who has successfully served a respondent at one address can later certify a completely different, outdated address under Rule 239a. The court has no mechanism to cross-check the certified address against other case records, returns of service, or publicly available information.

How This Affects SAPCR Cases

In Suits Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship (SAPCR), Rule 239a has particular consequences. When an attorney files a false Certificate of Last Known Address in a custody case:

  • The respondent parent never receives notice of the default judgment
  • The court enters orders regarding custody, conservatorship, and visitation based solely on the petitioner's claims
  • The respondent parent may not learn of the judgment for months or years
  • Children are separated from a parent without that parent ever having an opportunity to be heard

Documented Case: Cause No. 202417675

In Cause No. 202417675 in the 311th District Court of Harris County, Texas, the following events are documented in public court records:

  • On April 7, 2024, the respondent was personally served with a SAPCR petition at a Baytown, Texas hotel.
  • Between April 28 and May 9, 2024, the respondent sent three emails to the petitioner's attorney requesting service documents and mediation. No response was received.
  • On June 3, 2024, the respondent appeared in a related Habeas Corpus case in the same court, properly served at his actual address.
  • On July 19, 2024 — 103 days after successful personal service — the petitioner's attorney filed a Certificate of Last Known Address certifying an address the respondent had not occupied since December 31, 2013.
  • On August 20, 2024, a default judgment was entered based on service to the decade-old address.
  • The respondent did not learn of the judgment until October 16, 2025 — over 14 months later.

These facts are drawn from Harris County District Clerk public records, available at hcdistrictclerk.com. A fraud lawsuit (Cause No. 2025-95850) and a Bill of Review (Cause No. 2025-92876) have been filed. The fraud case is set for trial in February 2027.

How to Check If You Were Affected

  1. Search your county's district clerk website for cases involving your name.
  2. If a default judgment exists, request a copy of the Rule 239a Certificate of Last Known Address.
  3. Compare the certified address to where you actually lived at the time.
  4. Check whether a return of service was filed for any personal service.
  5. Gather documentation of your actual address (lease, utilities, mail, other court records).
  6. Consult a family law attorney about filing a Bill of Review. Time limits apply.

Proposed Reforms: The SAFE SAPCR Act

The SAFE SAPCR Act proposes closing the Rule 239a verification gap by requiring:

  • Mandatory constable service in SAPCR cases
  • Attorney due diligence certification documenting steps taken to locate the respondent
  • An automated case registry allowing parents to check if a SAPCR has been filed involving their children
  • Expedited Bill of Review hearings when extrinsic fraud is alleged
  • Penalties for knowingly filing a false Certificate of Last Known Address
