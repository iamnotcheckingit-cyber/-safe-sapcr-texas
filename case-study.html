Harris County Case Study

A documented timeline of how extrinsic fraud separates families

Disclaimer: This case study is based entirely on public court records filed in Harris County, Texas. All dates, names, and facts are sourced from official court filings. This is presented for educational and advocacy purposes to illustrate systemic issues requiring legislative reform.

Understanding What Happened

This case study documents a real SAPCR (Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship) case in Harris County, Texas. The facts presented here come directly from public court records and official filings. They illustrate the systemic vulnerabilities that allow extrinsic fraud to occur.

The purpose of this documentation is not to make accusations, but to show how the current system enables abuse and why legislative reform is necessary to protect families.

Timeline of Events (From Public Records)

April 6, 2024
Personal Service at Harris County Location

Respondent was personally served with SAPCR petition at a location in Harris County, Texas.

The petitioner's attorney, Laci Rendon, successfully located and served the respondent at this location.

Key Point

The attorney demonstrated the ability to locate and serve the respondent at his actual location. This proves they knew how to find him.

April 28, 30 & May 9, 2024
Three Emails Sent to Attorney - No Response

Respondent sent three emails to Laci Rendon at laci@rendonlegal.com:

  • April 28, 2024: Requested copies of service documents and asked about mediation
  • April 30, 2024: Follow-up email with no response
  • May 9, 2024: Third request for mediation, included contact phone number

No response was ever received to any of these emails.

Evidence of Knowledge

The attorney received emails with the respondent's contact information but did not respond. This proves continued awareness of how to reach him.

May 31 & June 8, 2024
Petitioner Visits Respondent at Known Locations

The petitioner (attorney's client) visited the respondent at locations in the Houston area on May 31, 2024 and June 8, 2024.

These visits occurred 49 and 41 days respectively before the false Certificate of Last Known Address was filed on July 19, 2024.

Direct Knowledge

The petitioner knew exactly where the respondent was located. This information was available to the attorney.

2023
Petitioner Files Habeas Corpus Petition

The petitioner filed a Habeas Corpus case in Harris County, involving the same parties and children. The respondent was properly served at his out-of-state address.

Proof of Correct Address

This concurrent case proves the petitioner and her attorney knew the respondent's correct address and could successfully serve him there—months before filing the false Certificate of Last Known Address in the SAPCR case.

June 4, 2024
Habeas Case Dismissed - Petitioner Fails to Appear

In the Habeas Corpus case, the respondent appeared in court as required. The petitioner failed to appear, and the case was Dismissed for Want of Prosecution (DWOP).

This dismissal occurred just 45 days before the attorney filed the false Certificate of Last Known Address certifying an outdated Harris County address.

Pattern of Knowledge

The petitioner knew the respondent's correct out-of-state address (service was successful), knew he would appear in court, yet chose not to appear herself. Just weeks later, her attorney filed a certificate claiming his last known address was in Harris County—an address abandoned over a decade earlier.

July 19, 2024
False Certificate of Last Known Address Filed

104 days after personally serving the respondent, and 45 days after the Habeas case was dismissed, attorney Laci Rendon filed a Certificate of Last Known Address certifying the respondent's address as a location in Harris County.

[Address redacted to protect third parties - an apartment in Harris County]

The respondent had not resided at this address at any time after December 31, 2013—over 10 years before the certificate was filed.

The False Certification

The attorney certified an address she knew to be false. She had personally arranged service 104 days earlier, received three emails, and her client had visited the respondent twice in recent weeks. The concurrent Habeas case showed successful service at his actual address.

August 20, 2024
Default Judgment Entered

Based on the false certificate, the court authorized service by mail to an address where the respondent had not lived in over a decade.

A default judgment was entered awarding sole managing conservatorship to the petitioner. The respondent had no knowledge this hearing occurred.

Constitutional Violation

The respondent was denied his fundamental constitutional right to be heard. His parental rights were determined through paperwork, not evidence, without any opportunity to participate.

October 16, 2025
First Notice of Judgment - 14 Months Later

The respondent learned of the default judgment for the first time when the petitioner (not the attorney) sent him a photograph of the Final Order.

This was over 14 months after the judgment was entered. During this time, the respondent was separated from his children without knowing why.

2025
Legal Action Filed

The respondent has filed:

  • Bill of Review to vacate the fraudulent default judgment
  • Civil Action for fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and filing a fraudulent court record
  • State Bar Grievance against the attorney

Legal Issues Raised

  • Rule 239a Exploitation: The current rule requires no verification of address accuracy before an attorney can certify it. Even when an attorney demonstrably knows the correct address, no mechanism prevents false certification.
  • Due Process Violation: The Fourteenth Amendment guarantees that no state shall deprive any person of liberty without due process of law. Custody rights are a fundamental liberty interest that cannot be taken without notice and an opportunity to be heard.
  • Potential Criminal Liability: Texas Penal Code §37.10 (Tampering with Governmental Record) may apply when an attorney knowingly makes a false entry in a court filing.
  • Inadequate Remedy: The Bill of Review process places the burden on fraud victims and requires resources most affected parents don't have. There is no expedited process for extrinsic fraud.
  • Child's Best Interest: Default judgments made without both parents' input cannot truly serve the child's best interest—a core principle of family law.

Why Existing Legislation (SB 2794) Doesn't Help

Some might ask: doesn't Texas already have laws requiring proper service? The answer is yes—but they have no teeth.

  • SB 2794 (2023): This bill added requirements for service of process, but it relies on constables and sheriffs to enforce service standards. When attorneys file false certificates, there is no mechanism for constables or sheriffs to verify the information or challenge it.
  • No Enforcement Mechanism: Constables serve papers at addresses provided to them. They cannot investigate whether an address is current or correct. The attorney's certification is taken at face value.
  • Sheriff Limitations: Sheriffs have no authority to question attorney certifications. They execute service as directed. If an attorney certifies a false address, the sheriff's office sends papers to that address without question.
  • The Gap: The current system assumes attorneys will act honestly. When they don't, there is no safety net—no verification, no challenge process, and no accountability until after the damage is done.

The SAFE SAPCR Act addresses this gap by requiring mandatory verification, certified mail with tracking, and expedited remedies when fraud is discovered.

This Must Change

The SAFE SAPCR Act would require mandatory constable service, attorney due diligence certification, and expedited remedies for fraud victims. No parent should lose their children through fraud.
